How Cooking Materials Respond to High Heat

A technical overview of how glass, stainless steel, and synthetic non-stick coatings perform under repeated heat exposure.

Why Heat Stability Matters in Air Fryers

Air fryers commonly operate between 300°F and 400°F. Over time, repeated exposure to elevated temperatures can affect the structural and surface properties of certain materials.

Evaluating heat stability helps determine long-term durability, surface integrity, and maintenance requirements. Materials that maintain their properties under thermal cycling provide better longevity and consistent cooking performance.

For health-conscious consumers, understanding how materials behave under heat is crucial for making informed decisions about cookware safety and durability.

Synthetic Non-Stick Coatings (PTFE-Based)

PTFE-based coatings are widely used in cookware because they provide low friction and easy food release. These coatings are engineered to withstand typical cooking temperatures up to approximately 500°F.

However, over extended use, physical abrasion, scratching, and repeated heating cycles may contribute to surface wear. Thermal degradation can occur if the coating is overheated, potentially affecting its non-stick properties.

Surface degradation primarily affects non-stick performance. Consumers concerned with long-term durability may prefer materials that do not rely on applied coatings. Regular inspection for signs of wear is recommended for coated cookware.

Tempered Glass

Tempered glass is chemically stable and does not require synthetic surface coatings for food contact. It is manufactured through thermal or chemical processes that increase its strength compared to regular glass.

Under normal cooking conditions, glass does not degrade, flake, or release surface treatments. It maintains its inert properties even after repeated heating and cooling cycles. Its primary vulnerability is mechanical impact, not heat instability or chemical degradation.

Borosilicate glass, in particular, offers excellent thermal shock resistance, making it suitable for cooking applications that involve rapid temperature changes.

Food-Grade Stainless Steel

Stainless steel used in food applications, particularly 304-grade (18/8) alloys, is valued for its corrosion resistance and structural integrity under heat. The chromium content forms a protective oxide layer that prevents rust and degradation.

Unlike coated materials, stainless steel does not rely on applied surface layers. Its durability is inherent to the alloy composition. It maintains its structural properties across a wide temperature range and does not warp under normal cooking conditions.

Stainless steel is also non-porous and does not react with acidic foods, making it a versatile choice for various cooking applications. With proper care, stainless steel cookware can last for decades.
