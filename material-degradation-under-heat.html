How Cooking Materials Respond to High Heat

A technical overview of how glass, stainless steel, and synthetic non-stick coatings perform under repeated heat exposure.

Why Heat Stability Matters in Air Fryers

Air fryers commonly operate between 300°F and 400°F. Over time, repeated exposure to elevated temperatures can affect the structural and surface properties of certain materials.

Evaluating heat stability helps determine long-term durability, surface integrity, and maintenance requirements.

Synthetic Non-Stick Coatings (PTFE-Based)

PTFE-based coatings are widely used in cookware because they provide low friction and easy food release.

These coatings are engineered to withstand typical cooking temperatures. However, over extended use, physical abrasion, scratching, and repeated heating cycles may contribute to surface wear.

Surface degradation primarily affects non-stick performance. Consumers concerned with long-term durability may prefer materials that do not rely on applied coatings.

Tempered Glass

Tempered glass is chemically stable and does not require synthetic surface coatings for food contact.

Under normal cooking conditions, glass does not degrade, flake, or release surface treatments. Its primary vulnerability is mechanical impact, not heat instability.

Food-Grade Stainless Steel

Stainless steel used in food applications, particularly 304-grade alloys, is valued for its corrosion resistance and structural integrity under heat.

Unlike coated materials, stainless steel does not rely on applied surface layers. Its durability is inherent to the alloy composition.
